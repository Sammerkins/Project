The American Dream

Cultural Context

This book has its origins in the author, Lorraine Hansberry's childhood. At a young age, she and her family moved into a previously all-white neighborhood. This right, won in a supreme court case, was met with massive resistance. All this translated into the play which takes place in the 1950s a decade before the civil rights movement took off. One source described it saying, “Beneath the economic prosperity that characterized America in the years following World War II roiled growing domestic and racial tension”(6). Another source described the decade as having made clear, “the gap between the American dream and the black Americans” (Domina). Both of these descriptions are made evident in this play. This charming tale shows the Younger Family, with each person trying to achieve their American dream.

Their Dreams

  • Mama - Her dream is to give her children a better life than she had. This shows through her giving Walter the money he needs to follow his dream and paying for Benetha’s school. Her second dream is one she has held onto for many years: having a house with a garden she can tend and a yard for her grandson, Travis, to play in.
  • Walter - His dream is to be wealthy enough to provide for his wife and son. His second dream is to make sure that Travis, his son, can reach his goals and dreams easier than he has been able to.
  • Ruth - Her dream is to have a happy family and for her son and husband's dreams to come true. The house becomes a dream for her because she believes that it will help her family be happy.
  • Beneatha - Her dream is to be able to help people. This shows through her wanting to be a doctor and learning all she can. Her second dream is to be free of what is expected of her.

Summary

Each of the Youngers has their own "American Dream." To each, the dream was to leave the world a better place for those they loved. Ultimately, the dream solidified into their buying and moving into a house.
